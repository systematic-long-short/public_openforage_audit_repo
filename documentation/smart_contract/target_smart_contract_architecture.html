This document began as the desired end-state of the smart-contract architecture. The contracts named here as new — USDCTreasury, FORAGETreasury, and the slimmed HLTradingBridge — now exist in openforage_smart_contracts/src/; the predecessor treasury contracts, HyperEVM trading stack, and CCIP transport are no longer active production source. Two consolidations drive the whole document: (1) HyperLiquid: 8 contracts → 1 (an Arbitrum-side custodian; zero HyperEVM contracts), with custody and trading moved off-chain (a single cold master account + a fund-less trading key); (2) Treasuries: ProtocolTreasury + FundingTreasury → USDCTreasury, and RewardPool + DepositorPool + Partnerships → FORAGETreasury. What you most need to know — in 60 seconds

  • What it is. A set of on-chain "vault" programs that take in dollars (USDC), hand them to a market-neutral trading operation, and share the profits and losses back to depositors by fixed rules no single person can override. That core is unchanged in the target.
  • What protects depositors. Every movement of money is still bounded by hard-coded caps; an emergency "brake" (the Guardian) can only ever slow things down, never move funds out; and any change to the rules sits in an 8-day public waiting room before it can take effect.
  • The tier idea. Unchanged. Four tiers; longer lock-up = bigger share of the profit; losses are shared pro-rata across all four tiers, the same percentage from each.
  • What changes (HyperLiquid). Trading no longer runs through an on-chain account on a second blockchain. Capital sits in a single cold custody account, a separate fund-less trading key places trades off-chain on HyperLiquid's native exchange, and a keeper reports the value back. One Arbitrum contract (the slimmed HLTradingBridge) holds the caps and accounting. The cross-chain bridge (Chainlink CCIP) is no longer needed.
  • What changes (treasuries). Five treasury contracts become two — one for all protocol USDC (the USDCTreasury money router), one for all treasury FORAGE. The FORAGE side is unchanged; the USDC side is redesigned — profit and loss are recognised as accounting at each NAV attestation (no cash moves), then returned USDC is earmarked and disbursed by purpose, separately (§4, §13).
  • The honest trade-off. Moving trading off-chain removes the on-chain, per-instruction gate that used to constrain the trading key. The desk's exposure is instead held by the same Arbitrum caps that already bound it, plus conservative valuation and the Guardian's powers. In return, trading gets materially faster (no on-chain transaction per order). §7 and §9 state this trade plainly.
  • Stage. Implemented on testnet This is the current target implementation in the repo and on Arbitrum Sepolia. It is not a mainnet deployment, and it still needs external audit sign-off before production launch.
§1

What this is, and why it exists

A programmable vault for a market-neutral trading fund — streamlined.

OpenForage is, at heart, an automated fund. Depositors put in dollars; the protocol deploys that capital to a trading operation that aims to make money regardless of which way markets move ("market-neutral"); and the gains or losses are distributed back to depositors automatically. None of that purpose changes in the target.

The difference from a traditional fund is that the rule-book is written as smart contracts — programs that live on a blockchain and execute exactly as written, the same way every time, with every action permanently recorded and publicly auditable. The contracts in this document are that rule-book.

Why a target document. The first build proved the system end-to-end, and in doing so showed where it was carrying more machinery than it needed. Two areas were judged over-engineered: an eight-contract cross-chain apparatus to make trading "trustless on-chain," and five separate treasury contracts that each did one job. This document is the agreed destination of simplifying both — fewer contracts to audit, deploy, and reason about, with the depositor protections kept intact. It answers the same four questions as before — how dollars get in and out, how capital reaches the desk and returns, how profit and loss are shared, and who may change anything — with a smaller surface.

The key idea is unchanged. At every hop a hard-coded cap limits how fast money can move. The target keeps every one of those caps — including the same deploy and return caps on the custodian that already bound the trading desk's exposure. What changed at steps 4–7 is where trading happens (off-chain, via a cold account and a fund-less key), how value comes back (a keeper posts the NAV instead of a cross-chain message), and that the treasury now separates recognising PnL (accounting, step 6) from moving the cash (returns and disbursement, step 7) — not the limits that contain it. Every USDC exit — vault redemption, the protocol-retained withdrawal, the Foundation disbursement, and agent pay — additionally carries a rolling-24-hour cash cap, so no single day can drain any of them.

The eight steps in words

  • 1 · Deposit. A depositor sends USDC to the Vault and receives an equal amount of RISKUSD, throttled per block, per day, and per week.
  • 2 · The Vault. The Vault holds the cash and keeps proving its solvency rule: cash on hand plus the conservative value of money out trading must always cover every receipt in existence.
  • 3 · Stake. The depositor stakes RISKUSD into one of four tiers. The tier sets the lock-up and the profit split; losses are shared pro-rata across all tiers (§5).
  • 4 · Deploy. The Vault sends up to 95% of its capital to the custodian (HLTradingBridge), always keeping at least a 5% buffer. The custodian forwards capital to the protocol's single cold master account on HyperLiquid, within the per-block and per-day deploy caps.
  • 5 · Trade. On HyperLiquid, a separate fund-less trading key places trades on the master account's behalf, signing orders off-chain on the native exchange. The trading key can trade but cannot withdraw — it holds no funds.
  • 6 · Settle (accounting only). A permissioned keeper reports the capital's current worth (NAV); the USDCTreasury recognises the profit or loss as accounting — no USDC moves. A profit marks the tier shares up immediately and records the split: depositors and agents keep their tier shares of profit (always 70% together), leaving a flat 30% protocol share, of which the Foundation takes 50% (15% of profit), and the protocol retains the other 50%. A loss marks the tier shares down pro-rata across all tiers, then the retained buffer absorbs any remainder.
  • 7 · Return & route (cash). Periodically the custodian sends real USDC back. Principal comes back through returnPrincipalUSDC straight to the Vault, refreshing the cash available to redeem. Profit comes back through returnPnLUSDC and is earmarked — a vault top-up for depositors, plus the agent, protocol, and Foundation cuts — and a generic disburse later pays each earmark to its allowed destination. The immediate mark-up in step 6 can lead the cash; the 5% weekly cap (fixed at week-start) and the conservative min(book, NAV) rule keep redemptions bounded and backed until it arrives.
  • 8 · Redeem. A depositor hands back the receipt and gets USDC out — no more than 5% of the total per week (and no more than ~2% in any rolling 24 hours), so a panic cannot drain the vault faster than capital can be recalled.
§5

The tiers and the economics

Unchanged: lock longer, earn a bigger share of the profit — losses are shared equally across all tiers.

The four tiers are the heart of the product. Think of them as profit-share classes: the longer you lock your money, the bigger the share of profit you keep. Of every dollar of profit, depositors keep their tier share (50–65%) and the agents are funded by the tier fee (20–5%); these always sum to 70%, so the protocol share is a flat 30% of profit across every tier. Out of that protocol share the OpenForage Foundation takes 50% — 15% of profit — and the protocol retains the other 50% (also 15% of profit); 50% is the hard cap and can never be raised higher. Losses, by contrast, are not tranched — they are shared pro-rata across all four tiers at once, so every tier takes the same percentage hit. A longer lock-up buys a bigger slice of the upside; it gives no extra protection on the downside.

How a loss is shared. If the desk loses money, the USDCTreasury reduces all four tiers at once, in proportion to how much each holds — so every tier takes the same percentage hit. There is no first-loss tier and no loss queue. Only if a loss is large enough to use up the tiers' capital does the protocol's own retained buffer absorb the remainder. (This is exactly today's behaviour, now living in USDCTreasury instead of ProtocolTreasury.)

The percentages above are the launch configuration set when the contracts are deployed; they can be changed later only through the governance process in §6. The Foundation's cut is 50% of the protocol share — 15% of profit — with 50% also the hard cap and no higher; the protocol retains the other 50% (15% of profit). The vault also opens with a $10M total-deposit cap.

Guardian authority — three lanes

The Guardian is not one power but three deliberately separated lanes. The principle behind the split is one line: guardians may restore governance control during an incident, but they may never enact a governance outcome themselves. Every lane either stops activity or hands control back to the normal vote — none of them lets guardians spend, mint, choose where funds go, or rewrite the rules.

Lane Who What it can do What it can never do
1 · Stop Any one guardian (1 of 7) Pause a whitelisted contract; freeze valuations and outbound deploys on the trading custodian — inbound returns stay open. Restrict-only. Unpause, un-freeze, move funds, or loosen anything.
2 · Recover A 4-of-7 guardian super-majority Cancel a malicious proposal; accelerate a governance-approved emergency change to a 10-minute floor; rotate a compromised guardian seat or a captured voting-delegation to its pre-committed successor. Originate a rotation of the custody account or the governor; accelerate a change to the pre-committed-successor list itself; touch funds or parameters.
3 · Govern Token-holder vote + 8-day timelock Everything else: unpause, un-freeze, rotate the custody account or the governor, upgrade contracts, change economic parameters, manage the successor list, supply control. Skip the 8-day wait — though a healthy 4-of-7 may accelerate most changes to 10 minutes (never the successor list, which always waits the full window).

The pre-committed successor list is a governance-maintained registry (preCommittedSuccessors[role], held on the GuardianModule — see §13) that names, in advance, the safe address each rotatable role (the cold-account successor, each guardian seat, each voting-delegation pool, and any designated large delegators) would move to in an emergency. Lane 2 can only ever rotate to a pre-committed address, which is exactly what stops the recovery power from becoming an attack. Changing the list itself is Lane 3 only and can never be accelerated.

Where this stands, stated honestly. This three-lane model has moved from target design into GuardianModule. The current contract still keeps the one-guardian fast lane for pause/cancel/emergency-execute/propose permissions, and it now also carries the 4-of-7 accelerated rotation machinery, the 10-minute acceleration floor, and the preCommittedSuccessor registry that keeps every recovery move pinned to a governance-prepared successor.

The honest ceiling. A 4-of-7 quorum is both the strength of the recovery lane and its limit: if four or more of the seven guardians are compromised together, deterministic on-chain recovery is gone — a liveness checkmate — and if governance is captured at the same time, it is full checkmate. That is why the seven seats are held by seven distinct trust zones with no one person owning four of them, and why the 10-minute acceleration floor presumes automated monitoring and a standing cancel responder. §7 states the residual risk in full.

The approved custody account, and how it rotates

Because capital now rests in a single cold master account off-chain, the trading custodian (the slimmed HLTradingBridge on Arbitrum) holds that account's address on-chain — it is the only place the Vault's capital may be sent, the only destination withdrawals may return to, and the account whose value the keeper attests. Rotating that address (for routine key hygiene, or to recover from a compromise) has three deliberate paths, mapping onto the three lanes above:

  • Routine rotation — token-holder vote + 8-day timelock (Lane 3). The normal, fully-deliberate path, identical to any other parameter change: the approved-account address is a governance-only change that lands after the 8-day wait, and it can only ever be set to the pre-committed cold-account successor.
  • Emergency freeze — any guardian, immediate (Lane 1). The Guardian (or owner) can instantly freeze deploys — the flow of capital out to the account — while a rotation is arranged. The freeze is asymmetric: returns (capital coming back to the Vault) stay open, because pulling capital home can only ever reduce exposure, never add to it — the one exception being the brief window while a loss is being recorded (see "Why the freeze is asymmetric" below). Freezing only ever restricts — it cannot move funds and never widens a cap.
  • Accelerated rotation — a 4-of-7 guardian super-majority (Lane 2). If a key is compromised and waiting 8 days is unsafe, governance still originates the rotation, but a four-of-seven guardian quorum can accelerate it to a 10-minute floor — and only to a pre-committed successor address. Guardians never repoint custody on their own initiative; they compress the timelock on a governance-approved switch to a pre-agreed safe address.

Why guardians cannot pick the new account. Letting a 4-of-7 quorum directly repoint custody would turn a guardian-quorum compromise into authority over the very account that receives the protocol's capital. Restricting Lane 2 to accelerating a governance-originated rotation to a pre-committed successor keeps the choice of custody target a governance decision while still allowing a same-day response — a guardian-quorum compromise can delay or accelerate, never choose, the new account. Custody itself remains a single off-chain cold account by design (see §9); the 4-of-7 is a threshold on the guardian role, never a multi-signer custody wallet.

Why the freeze is asymmetric

A freeze is not a blunt "halt everything." It follows one rule: block any flow that could harm the protocol; keep open any flow that can only help it. A deploy sends capital out toward an account that may be compromised, so a freeze stops it. A verified return brings capital back into the Vault and can only reduce exposure, so it stays open — subject to the same caps and checks that always apply (a freeze never widens a cap). The only pause a freeze adds to a return is the brief window while a loss is being recorded, so the loss bookkeeping cannot desync. Valuation-dependent flows freeze because they would apply the very price report the freeze exists to distrust.

The asymmetric freeze governs the custodian's capital flow and valuation directly. User-flow fairness during an incident is held by the conservative min(book, NAV) valuation and the unresolved-loss gate (which blocks stale-price exits), with a separate full Vault-pause available to governance if every user flow must stop — not by any widened guardian power. Like the three-lane model above, this directional freeze is now implemented in the slimmed HLTradingBridge: deploys and positive NAV movement are frozen, while principal/PnL return paths and loss recording remain available for incident resolution.

§7

How it's kept safe

The same layered controls — plus an honest account of the one protection the off-chain move trades away.

Safety here is layers, not one feature. The target keeps the Vault's controls unchanged and keeps the caps that bound the trading desk. Four representative controls convey the philosophy; the callout after them states, plainly, the one thing the HyperLiquid simplification gives up and what holds the line instead.

The solvency promise

On every deposit, redemption, and capital deployment, the Vault re-checks a single rule and refuses the action if it would break:

"Cash in the vault, plus the conservative value of money out trading, must always cover every dollar-receipt in existence."

In code that is vaultUSDC + min(bookValue, NAV) ≥ total RISKUSD — the min() means deployed capital is always valued cautiously. Unchanged in the target.

The shrink-only brake

The Guardian can tighten caps, pause, or freeze — its only direction is more restrictive. It cannot widen a limit, mint, or move funds. Its freeze is asymmetric by direction: it stops the harmful outbound flow (deploys) while leaving the beneficial inbound flow (verified returns) open at its existing caps — still restrict-only, because keeping an already-capped return path available adds no new capability and widens nothing (§6). The target adds one new restrictive-or-recovery power: a 4-of-7 guardian super-majority can accelerate a governance-approved custody rotation in a compromise (§6).

Bounded value reports

The keeper's NAV is not trusted blindly. An upward report can raise the recorded value by at most ~10% per interval; a downward (loss) report is deliberately not capped — it registers immediately and triggers loss handling and exit locks. A stale report is rejected rather than used, the Guardian can freeze attestations, and the conservative min(book, NAV) rule applies on top.

Bounded capital flow

The custodian keeps the same hard caps as today: a per-block and per-day ceiling on how much capital can be deployed, and a per-call and per-day ceiling on how much can be returned. These are the limits that contain the off-chain desk's exposure.

The honest trade-off — what moving trading off-chain gives up

What is removed. Today, trades are submitted through an on-chain account on HyperLiquid that re-checks every single instruction against an allow-list and a forbidden-action gate before it reaches the exchange. That made the desk's trading trustless on-chain: even the trading key could not place a disallowed action. The target removes that on-chain, per-instruction gate.

What holds the line instead. The desk's exposure is bounded by the same Arbitrum caps that already limited it — how much capital can be deployed to the account and how much can be pulled back, per block and per day — together with the conservative NAV valuation, the Guardian's freeze, and the ability to rotate the account. The trading key is fund-less: it can trade the deployed capital but can never withdraw it. So a compromised trading key can lose, at most, what is already deployed (itself capped), and cannot exfiltrate funds.

The residual risk, stated plainly. The cold master account is a trusted custody root. It is an off-chain key, so the on-chain caps cannot physically force it to send only to the Vault — a compromise of the cold key can withdraw the capital currently deployed to it. What contains that: only a fraction of the vault is ever deployed (the ≥5% buffer stays on-chain), the per-block and per-day deploy caps limit how fast capital can accumulate at the account, conservative valuation surfaces any shortfall as a loss rather than hiding it, that loss is socialised pro-rata, a guardian can instantly freeze further deploys, and a 4-of-7 guardian quorum can accelerate a vote-approved rotation to a fresh pre-committed account within minutes. The protocol's own withdrawal control adds to this: the keeper validates every withdrawal against a capped, destination-pinned on-chain intent record before it signs, and the return path reconciles arrivals by a real balance delta — a keeper-side policy plus on-chain detection, since the chain cannot itself veto an off-chain signature (§9, §13). Funds already at a compromised account are nonetheless at risk — this is the residual the off-chain model accepts; it can be hardened further still with HyperLiquid-level withdrawal-address allow-listing, were one offered (an operational control, not assumed here).

What is gained. Trading becomes materially faster. On-chain submission meant an on-chain transaction — block time, gas, and an on-chain re-check — for every order. Off-chain, the fund-less key signs orders directly on HyperLiquid's native exchange, the path built for low-latency trading. The speed gain comes from off-chain execution, not from the cold account (which only ever holds and moves custody, never trades).

The guardian-authority residual. The 4-of-7 recovery lane (§6) is what lets honest guardians cancel a hostile proposal and accelerate a fix during an incident — but it is also the protocol's recovery ceiling. If four or more of the seven guardians are compromised together, a hostile quorum can keep cancelling rotations and unpause proposals, and deterministic on-chain recovery is gone — a liveness checkmate. If governance is captured at the same time, it is full checkmate. Two disciplines hold the line below that threshold: the seven seats sit in seven distinct trust zones with no person owning four, and the 10-minute acceleration floor presumes automated monitoring plus a standing cancel responder. One distinction matters: the guardian lanes themselves can never move funds, so a 4-of-7 guardian compromise on its own is a liveness failure, not a drain — guardians can stall or accelerate a change, never originate a fund-moving one. The drain case is the combined one: a hostile governance majority plus a hostile 4-of-7 quorum, where governance originates a fund-moving change and the quorum accelerates it to minutes. Keeping the vote and the guardian seats in separate hands is what makes that collusion — not a guardian-only compromise — the real worst case.

The accounting-ahead-of-cash window, stated plainly. In the target the USDCTreasury recognises profit as accounting the moment the keeper attests — the tier shares are marked up before the matching USDC has physically returned from the desk. That is deliberate, and two things bound it. First, redemptions are capped at 5% of supply per week, fixed at the start of the week, so even a large mark-up cannot be withdrawn faster than capital can be recalled — the cap is set before the attestation it would respond to. Second, the Vault's solvency rule values deployed capital conservatively (vaultUSDC + min(book, NAV) ≥ total RISKUSD), so a claim is always measured against the cautious value of what is actually out trading. The cash that ultimately backs the marked-up claim arrives separately, as the Vault top-up in the return step (§4 step 7) — it funds the claim, it does not create new value.

Other controls in the same family are unchanged: the trading custodian can never be over-funded (the 95% / 5%-buffer rule), tier share-values can never tick down from a yield update, the most sensitive settings use two-step setters, and a blocklist plus a custodian registry constrain exactly which addresses can ever be trusted.

atRISKUSD — the four tier shares

atRISKUSD · Arbitrum · live · deployed 4× (Tiers 0–3) · unchanged in the target

The profit-share class a depositor holds after staking — one contract per tier, four in all.

What it does

When a depositor stakes their RISKUSD they receive a tier share: an interest-bearing vault share (the standard ERC-4626 recipe) whose value rises as trading profit is paid in. The tier they pick sets two things — how long the money is locked, and how big a slice of the profit they keep. Longer lock, bigger slice. A share's value can never tick down from a profit update; it only falls when a real loss is shared. Two "never down" rules are at work and should not be confused: a profit update never lowers a share's value, and an exit never lowers the backing-per-share of the holders who stay. Losses are not tranched here — every tier takes the same percentage hit at once (see §5).

Two further rules govern a tier share once it exists. At lock-up maturity the position does not silently sit idle: by default it auto-renews for another full lock-up period (the depositor keeps their tier and its higher profit share), while a depositor who turns auto-renew off has their position reverted to Tier 0 (the no-lock tier) at maturity instead. Auto-renew is on by default; the depositor toggles it, and the staking queue drives the renew-or-revert pass (a permissionless batch, with a depositor self-revert fallback — see StakingQueue above). Transfers of the tier share are allowed but gated: a holder cannot transfer while still inside their lock-up window, a blocked address can neither send nor receive, and a holder who let an opted-out lock-up lapse may only send to the vault itself (to redeem) — not to a third party. The lock-up is tied to the address, fixed when the depositor stakes; it does not travel with the token, so once a holder is past their own lock-up the shares they send arrive unlocked. (A pending loss locks exits, not peer-to-peer transfers.)

What it can do

  • Accept a stake of RISKUSD and issue tier shares — via the staking queue.
  • Accrue profit, raising every share's value — paid in by the treasury; never decreases a share.
  • Absorb a loss, lowering every share's value pro-rata — the treasury, equal % across all four tiers.
  • Release a withdrawal after the lock-up and a cooldown, within a weekly cap — the depositor; it burns the leaver's own shares and never lowers the backing-per-share of the holders who stay (the payout is capped at the share value snapshotted when the withdrawal was requested, so a leaver takes no profit accrued during the cooldown — it stays with the stayers).
  • Lock exits while a loss settles — so no one races out ahead of a loss being shared.
  • Renew or revert a matured lock-up — renew the tier by default, or revert an opted-out position to Tier 0 — driven by the staking queue's permissionless expiry pass.
  • Transfer a tier share — once past the holder's own lock-up — any holder; blocked during lock-up and for blocked addresses; the lock does not travel with the token.

USDCTreasury — the protocol's money router (implemented — merges 2)

USDCTreasury · Arbitrum · implemented target · merges ProtocolTreasury + FundingTreasury

The money router for all protocol USDC: it recognises profit and loss as accounting on each attestation (no cash moves), then earmarks and disburses the actual USDC the desk returns — to the Vault, the agents, the Foundation, and the protocol's own buffer.

What it does

One contract for all protocol USDC, with two clearly separated jobs.

① Accounting — on each NAV attestation, no USDC moves. When the keeper reports value, the treasury recognises the period's profit or loss as pure accounting. A profit marks the four tier shares up immediately (this is the depositor's claim) and records the agent, protocol, and Foundation amounts; a loss marks the tier shares down pro-rata — the same percentage from each — and only if a loss is large enough to exhaust the tiers does the retained buffer absorb the remainder. No dollars are transferred in this step.

② Cash — when the desk actually returns USDC. Returned capital arrives by two functions. returnPrincipalUSDC sends principal straight to the Vault, refreshing the cash available for redemptions — it is never split to agents, protocol, or Foundation. returnPnLUSDC brings back realised profit, which is split into four earmark balances: a Vault top-up that funds the depositor claim already created in step ①; the agent lane; the protocol share; and the Foundation's cut of that protocol share. Earmark balances only ever hold USDC that has actually been returned.

③ Disbursement — a generic, purpose-keyed disburse. It pays an earmark out to its allowed destination and can never exceed the balance on hand: the Vault top-up goes to the RISKUSDVault; agent pay goes to any (non-blocked) agent wallet under per-payment and rolling-24-hour caps; the protocol and Foundation earmarks go only to an allow-listed primary wallet, each with a pre-set failover backup usable only when the primary is blocked or unavailable — and each is itself bounded by a rolling-24-hour cash cap, so no single day can drain it.

The depositor mark-up is not double-counted. Step ① raises the tier share value and defines the depositor's redemption claim; the step-② Vault top-up only funds the cash to honour it — it never raises share value a second time. A principal return refreshes redemption liquidity; it does not "fund profit".

How the split reads. Depositors keep their tier share of profit (50 / 55 / 60 / 65%) and the agents are funded by the tier fee (20 / 15 / 10 / 5%); the two always sum to 70%, so the protocol share is a flat 30% of profit in every tier. The Foundation takes 50% of that protocol share (15% of profit) and the protocol retains the other 50% (15% of profit); 50% is also the hard cap — never higher. Worked through $100 of Tier-0 profit: $50 depositor, $20 agent, $30 protocol share — of which $15 Foundation and $15 retained. When several tiers participate, each pool is shared between them in proportion to the matching tier weights.

Who is eligible is computed off-chain. The treasury pays an address; it carries no on-chain notion of a "contributor" or of who earned what. Agent eligibility and the amount owed are computed off-chain by the protocol's attribution, and governance publishes the result — the per-payment disburse list for cash, and a Merkle root for FORAGE rewards (the FORAGETreasury). This is a deliberate, accepted boundary: the on-chain surface is the payment and its caps, not the eligibility logic.

  • Disburse an earmark by purpose — vault top-up, agent pay, protocol spend, Foundation — never exceeding its balance — an authorised caller; blocklist + caps gate every send.

StakingQueue — the staking line

StakingQueue · Arbitrum · live · unchanged in the target

The orderly line that turns RISKUSD deposits into tier shares — fair, capped, and predictable.

What it does

Staking does not happen instantly; it goes through a two-lane first-in-first-out line. A standard lane and a priority lane keep the order fair and predictable, each tier has its own deposit-capacity cap, and a depositor can move up a tier from within the queue. Where a deposit's handling depends on the FORAGE price, the queue reads it with a staleness guard so a stale price cannot be used. FORAGE pricing here is dual-mode: by default the protocol sets a fixed FORAGE price by governance — the launch choice, since FORAGE may be too thinly traded early for an oracle feed — and governance can later switch to reading a live price from an oracle instead. Either way the price has one narrow job: it sizes how much FORAGE a depositor must lock to use the priority lane. A missing, zero, or stale price simply closes the priority lane and the deposit falls back to the standard lane; it never blocks the deposit.

Pricing mode Where the price comes from Freshness rule
Fixed price (launch default) Set by governance, within a hard upper bound; chosen for launch because FORAGE may be too thinly traded for an oracle feed. A nonzero fixed price is rejected once it is more than 7 days old; a price of zero turns the priority lane off entirely.
Oracle A live market price read from an external oracle, available only once governance has configured one. Rejected once older than a governance-set limit (at most 30 days); a bad reading is ignored, not treated as fatal.

Switching between the two modes is a two-step governance action that waits a fixed delay before it takes effect, and the protocol cannot switch to oracle mode until an oracle has actually been configured. In either mode, a price that is missing, zero, or stale only closes the priority lane — the deposit still proceeds in the standard lane.

Two lock lifecycles run alongside the line, and neither ever consumes a depositor's tokens. Priority-lane FORAGE is a refundable escrow lock, not a fee: the FORAGE stays in the depositor's own wallet but is held non-transferable (for the amount the price sized) while the queue entry is open, and it is released back in full the moment the entry is processed, cancelled, or admin-cancelled — never burned, never sent to a treasury. A release that fails is retried by a permissionless call, so the FORAGE can never be stranded. Tier lock-up maturity is driven from here too: when a staked position's lock-up (0 / 90 / 180 / 360 days, by tier) expires, the expiry pass renews it for another full period by default, or — for a depositor who has turned auto-renew off — reverts the position to Tier 0 (the no-lock tier). The batch pass is permissionless (an off-chain keeper normally drives it); a depositor who has opted out can also self-trigger their own reversion. The renew-vs-revert mechanics live on the tier share itself (see atRISKUSD below).

  • Accept a deposit of RISKUSD into a chosen tier and lane — any depositor; the RISKUSD entry and any priority-lane FORAGE escrow are held while queued and returned in full on processing or cancel.
  • Process the queue into tier-share deposits in order — an operator runs the batch; releases the entry's FORAGE escrow.
  • Cancel a queued entry and reclaim the RISKUSD + FORAGE escrow — the depositor (or an admin, returning both to the depositor).
  • Move a queued depositor up a tier — the depositor.
  • Process expired tier lock-ups — renew (default) or revert opted-out positions to Tier 0 — a permissionless batch anyone may run; a depositor may self-revert their own.
  • Tighten a tier's deposit cap in an emergency — a guardian (caps only shrink).
  • Pause intake — owner / governance.

HLTradingBridge · Arbitrum · implemented target · slimmed

The Vault's trading custodian — the one on-chain contract that sends capital to the off-chain desk and brings the value back.

What it does

The one remaining contract of the HyperLiquid stack, and it stays on Arbitrum. It holds the approved cold-account address, sends capital out and brings it back within the caps, accepts the keeper's NAV, records the value into the Vault for the USDCTreasury to crystallise the profit or loss as accounting, and returns USDC within the caps — principal to the Vault, realised profit to the USDCTreasury. A loss report still locks tier exits and is absorbed before deployed principal is reduced. The target build removed the cross-chain machinery: this slimmed version drops CCIP and the HyperEVM peer and takes NAV from a permissioned keeper instead.

  • Return USDC — principal to the Vault (returnPrincipalUSDC), realised profit to the USDCTreasury (returnPnLUSDC) — the executor.

Off-chain custody & the bridge — how capital actually crosses to HyperLiquid

not a contract — operated keys + HyperLiquid's native bridge

How capital actually reaches the trading desk: a single cold account on both chains, HyperLiquid's own bridge, and a fund-less key that trades but cannot withdraw.

What it does

This is the answer to "what is the best way to bridge funds from Arbitrum to HyperLiquid," and the short version is: use HyperLiquid's own native bridge (Bridge2), and let the single-account custody model do the hard part for you. Capital rests in one cold master account — an ordinary externally-owned key — whose address is the same on Arbitrum and on HyperLiquid. The Vault deploys USDC (within the caps above) to that account on Arbitrum; the account sends native USDC to Bridge2; and because Bridge2 credits the sender's own address on HyperLiquid, the money lands in that same account's trading balance in under a minute. A separate fund-less "agent" key then trades on its behalf — it can place orders but holds no funds and cannot withdraw.

This is why the single-account model is the enabler, not just a simplification: the old design needed funds credited to an on-chain contract, which HyperLiquid's bridge does not support (it credits the sender), and HyperLiquid also rejects withdrawals by contract accounts outright — which is why a real key was always required. With one account on both sides, the deposit "just works" and the return is a normal withdrawal the account signs itself.

Two operational traps to hard-guard

Native USDC only. Bridge2 credits native USDC; sending the older bridged "USDC.e" is not credited and can be lost. Five-USDC minimum per deposit, below which it is lost — so deposits are batched well above the floor. Deposits cost only Arbitrum gas; a withdrawal costs a flat ~1 USDC and arrives in a few minutes. The canonical Bridge2 address must be pinned from the official HyperLiquid app at integration time (this design document, being pre-mainnet, quotes no addresses).

Bridge2 is the bridge — kept swappable as a contingency

Bridge2 is the chosen route, and there is no planned migration off it and no migration machinery to build. The one forward-looking note is purely defensive: HyperLiquid has stated the Arbitrum Bridge2 may eventually be deprecated in favour of natively-minted USDC, so the deposit destination is kept governance-swappable — the CustodianRegistry already abstracts it — and could be re-pointed under timelock to whatever native route HyperLiquid then supports (Circle's native USDC / CCTP being the likely candidate) if and only if that ever becomes necessary. Treat that as a contingency, not a roadmap item. The other routes are not contenders for this lane either: Chainlink CCIP (removed here — it carries messages, it does not fund a trading balance) and third-party bridges (LayerZero / Stargate, Across, deBridge) serve multi-chain onboarding or non-USDC assets, not moving Arbitrum USDC into the same account's HyperLiquid balance.

The residual the model accepts (cross-ref §7)

The cold account is a trusted custody root: it is an off-chain key, so the on-chain caps cannot physically force it to send only to the Vault, and a HyperLiquid withdrawal lets the holder name any destination — so a compromise of that key can move the funds currently deployed. There is no HyperLiquid-enforced withdrawal allow-list, and — stated plainly — the chain cannot enforce the off-chain withdrawal signature at all: a key-holder can sign a HyperLiquid withdraw3 whether or not it matches anything on-chain, and the on-chain "intent" record only marks itself settled — it never moves the funds. What the protocol relies on instead is a layered control, honest about its limits: (1) the keeper validates each withdrawal against a capped, destination-pinned on-chain withdrawal-intent record (recipient = the Vault custodian) before it signs — a keeper-side policy, not a chain-enforced gate; (2) the return path reconciles arrivals by a real on-chain balance delta, so an out-of-band withdrawal cannot be laundered back as a "return" and shows up as a missing arrival; (3) monitoring and a standing incident response watch for an intent-less or mismatched movement. A related gap: the Vault records the deploy and hands USDC to the account but cannot force the off-chain step that bridges it; if the funds never arrive, the conservative NAV valuation surfaces the shortfall as a loss rather than hiding it. The irreducible residual — a compromised key signing a withdrawal the chain cannot veto — is what the rest contains: only a fraction is ever deployed (the ≥5% buffer stays on-chain), the per-block / per-day caps bound how fast capital accumulates, losses are socialised pro-rata, a guardian can instantly freeze deploys, and governance can rotate the account — a vote-approved rotation a 4-of-7 guardian quorum can accelerate to minutes (§6, §7).
