What you most need to know — in 60 seconds

  • What it is. A set of on-chain "vault" programs that take in dollars (USDC), hand them to a market-neutral trading operation, and share the profits and losses back to depositors by fixed rules no single person can override.
  • What protects depositors. Every movement of money is bounded by hard-coded caps; an emergency "brake" (the Guardian) can only ever slow things down, never speed them up or move funds out; and any change to the rules sits in an 8-day public waiting room before it can take effect.
  • The tier idea. Depositors pick one of four tiers. Longer lock-up = bigger share of the profits. Losses work differently: they are shared pro-rata across all four tiers — every tier takes the same percentage hit at once, so a longer lock-up buys more upside, not loss protection.
  • Where trading happens. Capital is deployed to a trading desk that operates on HyperLiquid; the two blockchains coordinate over an audited industry bridge (Chainlink CCIP).
  • Stage. Pre-mainnet The system runs and is heavily tested on test networks; it is not yet live on mainnet. Independently reviewed; no open critical or high-severity findings in the latest internal review.
  • Size at launch. The vault is capped at $10M of deposits to start.
§1

What this is, and why it exists

A programmable vault for a market-neutral trading fund.

OpenForage is, at heart, an automated fund. Depositors put in dollars; the protocol deploys that capital to a trading operation that aims to make money regardless of which way markets move ("market-neutral"); and the gains or losses are distributed back to depositors automatically.

The difference from a traditional fund is that the rule-book is written as smart contracts — programs that live on a blockchain and execute exactly as written, the same way every time, with every action permanently recorded and publicly auditable. Nobody can quietly change a payout, skim a balance, or move money in a way the code does not allow. The contracts in this document are that rule-book.

The job of the rule-book is to answer four questions safely and automatically: (1) how do dollars get in and out; (2) how does capital reach the trading desk and come back; (3) how are profits and losses shared; and (4) who is allowed to change anything, and under what limits. The sections below walk each of those in turn.

§2

The system at a glance

24 contracts, two blockchains, four jobs.

There are 24 production contracts. That sounds like a lot, but they fall into just four jobs, spread across two blockchains: Arbitrum (where the money, accounting and governance live — 16 contracts) and HyperLiquid (where the actual trading happens — 8 contracts). You only need to hold the four jobs in your head:

① Tokens & capital

The dollars come in here and become tradeable units. The Vault holds the cash; RISKUSD is the dollar receipt; the four atRISKUSD tiers are the staking options depositors choose.

② Treasury & distribution

Where profit and loss are routed. The USDCTreasury shares gains to the tiers and absorbs losses; the pools and staking queue handle rewards and token distribution.

③ Governance & safety

Who is allowed to change anything. The Governor runs token-holder votes; the Guardian is the emergency brake; registries and the blocklist enforce who/what is trusted.

④ The trading engine

The HyperLiquid side. Capital is bridged over, traded, and the resulting value is reported back. The two chains talk over Chainlink CCIP, an audited cross-chain messaging service.

The key idea. At every single hop, a hard-coded cap limits how fast money can move — how fast it can be created, how much can leave for trading, and how much can be withdrawn in a week. In normal operation no human can widen these limits on the spot; loosening any of them requires a token-holder vote and the 8-day delay (§6).

The seven steps in words

  • 1 · Deposit. A depositor sends USDC to the Vault and receives an equal amount of RISKUSD. To stop a runaway or an exploit minting unlimited receipts, the Vault throttles how much can be created per block, per day, and per week.
  • 2 · The Vault. The Vault is the cash reserve. It now holds the USDC and has issued matching RISKUSD, and from this moment it keeps proving a solvency rule: the cash on hand plus the conservative value of money out trading must always cover every receipt in existence.
  • 3 · Stake. The depositor stakes RISKUSD into one of four tiers and gets tier shares. The tier sets the lock-up and the profit split; losses are shared pro-rata across all tiers, the same for every tier (§5).
  • 4 · Deploy. The Vault sends up to 95% of its capital to the trading desk (the "custodian"), always keeping at least a 5% buffer on hand so routine withdrawals never wait on the trading floor.
  • 5 · Trade. On HyperLiquid, the desk runs the market-neutral strategy. The capital's current worth is periodically reported back to the Vault as a "NAV" (net asset value) attestation.
  • 6 · Settle. Gains and losses flow to the USDCTreasury, which shares profit to the tiers by their split (and takes the protocol fee), or spreads a loss across all tiers in proportion to their assets.
  • 7 · Redeem. A depositor hands back the receipt and gets USDC out — but no more than 5% of the total can be redeemed in any one week, so a panic cannot drain the vault faster than capital can be recalled from trading.
§5

The tiers and the economics

Four choices on one dial: lock longer, earn a bigger share of the profit — losses are shared equally across all tiers.

The four tiers are the heart of the product. Think of them as profit-share classes: the longer you lock your money, the bigger the share of profit you keep. Losses, by contrast, are not tranched — they are shared pro-rata across all four tiers at once, so every tier takes the same percentage hit. A longer lock-up buys a bigger slice of the upside; it gives no extra protection on the downside, because there is no loss queue.

  Tier 0Tier 1Tier 2Tier 3
Lock-up (your money is committed)None90 days180 days360 days
Your share of the profit50%55%60%65%
Protocol fee (the rest)20%15%10%5%
Loss sharing (if the desk loses money)Equal %Equal %Equal %Equal %

How a loss is shared. If the desk loses money, the USDCTreasury reduces all four tiers at once, in proportion to how much each holds — so every tier, from Tier 0 to Tier 3, takes the same percentage hit. There is no first-loss tier and no loss queue: a longer lock-up earns a bigger share of the profit, but gives no extra protection when the desk loses. Only if a loss is large enough to use up the tiers' capital does the protocol's own retained buffer absorb the remainder.

The percentages above are the launch configuration set when the contracts are deployed; they can be changed later only through the governance process in §6. The vault also opens with a $10M total-deposit cap.

§6

Governance — who has the keys

A token-holder vote, behind a mandatory 8-day waiting period.

The natural question for any fund is "who can touch the money or change the rules?" For OpenForage the answer is a chain of checks, with a deliberate delay built in so nothing can change by surprise.

Forward pointer. This page is the as-is architecture — what is deployed and tested today, where each guardian holds individual permission bits (pause, cancel, emergency, propose) and there is no several-of-many threshold: a single permitted guardian acts alone. A planned redesign reshapes guardian authority into a three-lane model (single-guardian stop · a 4-of-7 recovery quorum that accelerates governance, never replaces it · governance) with a pre-committed successor list, alongside moving trading off-chain. That is described in the companion target_smart_contract_architecture.html (§6) and is not yet built.

   ──────────────   ──▶  ───────────────    ──▶    ──────────────────   ──▶   ─────────────
   propose & vote        tallies the vote          holds the approved        change takes
   (need 1% to            (need 4% quorum,          change for 8 days          effect only
    propose)               5-day voting)            in public view             after the wait

Even a winning vote cannot change the system instantly. An approved change is parked in the timelock — a glass-walled waiting room — for 8 days before it can execute. Anyone who disagrees can see it coming and exit first. This is what makes "upgradeable" contracts safe rather than a back door.

Can the contracts be changed after launch?

Most contracts are upgradeable — their logic can be improved later — but only through the vote-plus-8-day-timelock path above, never unilaterally. A few things are deliberately fixed: the 1-for-1 dollar backing, the four-tier structure, and the safety invariants. One contract, the vesting wallet for team/partner tokens, is not upgradeable at all by design, so a vesting schedule cannot be quietly rewritten.

§7

How it's kept safe

Roughly 46 documented controls, layered so one failure is caught by the next.

Safety here is not one feature but layers — the contracts carry around 46 documented defence-in-depth controls (catalogued internally as R-1 through R-46). You do not need the list; four representative ones convey the philosophy.

The solvency promise

On every deposit, redemption, and capital deployment, the Vault re-checks a single rule and refuses the action if it would break:

"Cash in the vault, plus the conservative value of money out trading, must always cover every dollar-receipt in existence."

In code that is vaultUSDC + min(bookValue, NAV) ≥ total RISKUSD — the min() means the system always values deployed capital cautiously.

The shrink-only brake

The Guardian is like a fuse box that can trip a breaker to cut power but is physically incapable of pushing more current through the wires. In an emergency it can tighten caps, pause, or freeze — its only direction is more restrictive. It cannot widen a limit, mint, or move funds.

Speed limits on creation

New dollar-receipts can only be minted so fast — throttled per block, per day, and per week. A bug or an attacker cannot conjure unlimited supply in one transaction; the throttle buys time for the brake to engage.

Two-key changes

The most sensitive settings use two-step setters: a change must be proposed and then confirmed in a separate step, so a single fat-fingered or malicious transaction cannot flip a critical parameter.

Other controls in the same family: the trading desk can never be over-funded (the 95% / 5%-buffer rule), a stale or missing value report from the trading floor is automatically treated conservatively, tier share-values can never tick down from a yield update, and a blocklist plus a custodian registry constrain exactly which addresses can ever be trusted.

§9

The cross-chain trading engine — HyperLiquid (8 contracts)

Why a second blockchain exists, and the eight contracts that run the trading floor.

The accounting and governance live on Arbitrum, but the actual trading happens on HyperLiquid — a blockchain purpose-built for fast derivatives trading. So OpenForage spans two chains, and the interesting engineering is keeping them honest with each other.

The two halves never trust a raw message. Governance commands and value reports cross between them over Chainlink CCIP, an established cross-chain messaging service, and the receiving side only accepts a fixed, pre-approved set of instructions. Picture a diplomatic pouch on a vetted courier: only certain documents are allowed inside, and the recipient checks the seal.

§10

Maturity and status

Heavily tested, independently reviewed — and not yet on mainnet.

Pre-mainnet. These contracts are not yet deployed to a live (mainnet) network. They run and are exercised on local and test networks only. No real depositor funds are at stake today; "going live" is a separate, later step.

Tested

The codebase carries a large automated test suite — on the order of 4,100 passing tests across ~500 suites, including "fuzzing" (throwing thousands of random inputs at the code) and "invariant" testing (continuously checking the core promises hold). The contracts compile cleanly and pass automated security scanners.

Reviewed

The contracts have been independently reviewed by multiple external parties in addition to repeated internal audits. The most recent internal review cycle closed with no open critical, high, or medium-severity findings. External auditor sign-off is part of the path to mainnet.

In short: the architecture is complete and exercised end-to-end on test networks, with a clean recent review. What remains before real money flows is external audit sign-off and the mainnet deployment itself.
